### The 1000x discrepancy — DIAGNOSED (this is confound (a), not bug (b))

**Finding 1 — CRITICAL / high confidence — the endurance fixture is a different orbital regime (MEO), not the verified multisat LEO constellation.**
`endurance.rs:617` `shells = [(55.0, 2.0056), (56.0, 2.0060), (64.8, 2.1310)]` — mean motion ~2 rev/day = **~20,000 km altitude, GPS-MEO, 12 h period**. The VERIFIED multisat fixture (`multisat.rs:576`) is `[(53.0,15.064),(87.9,13.158),(86.4,14.342)]` = **~550–1200 km LEO, ~1.6 h period** (matches its own FixtureDescription). Verify: T=24/n hours; a=(µ(T/2π)²)^⅓. The MEO satellites sweep the sky **~7.5× slower** (0.50 vs 3.3–3.8 deg/min central rate). Doppler-only position observability comes almost entirely from geometry *change over the leg*; a 7.5× slower constellation is 7.5× weaker per unit time. That is the mechanism for 156 km vs 116 m at the same N=8. The 156 km is "correct" for this crippled slow geometry — it is **not** a bug, unit error, or injected drift (the `predict`/filter/error math is shared library code identical to multisat, and reproduces byte-for-byte). Note also the `constellation(id)` labels still say Starlink/OneWeb/Iridium (`endurance.rs:653`) — all LEO constellations — so the MEO orbits are physically mislabeled and represent no real system.

**Finding 2 — CRITICAL / high confidence — geometry is not held fixed and is not even measured.**
`endurance.rs:417` `visible_cohort` selects the **lowest-ID 8 visible sats at every epoch, with handovers** (vs multisat `persistent_cohort` at `multisat.rs:307`, one fixed cohort, provably no handover). The endurance study **dropped the `gdop()` instrumentation** multisat had (`multisat.rs:531`) — it reports **no GDOP number at all**, only a prose `geometry` string. So the study cannot demonstrate its geometry is comparable to multisat's 1.8, nor that it is held constant across tiers. In fact it is not: the leg tiers end at different epochs and accumulate different handovers (1.0→3.0 in the results table), so the leg sweep varies geometry, handover count, and convergence time simultaneously. Answer to the brief's question "report the GDOP the endurance study achieves vs 1.8": **it doesn't compute one** — a material instrumentation gap for a geometry-controlled claim.

**Finding 3 — HIGH / high confidence — the clock sweep is inconclusive-by-construction (confound (c) confirmed).** The Rb-vs-OCXO delta is 1.3 m sitting on a 114–157 km geometry error (0.0008%). Worse, the injected drift (`endurance.rs:378` `clock_fractional * c`) is a **constant common-mode** offset identical across all SVs — precisely what a receiver-clock nuisance state absorbs — so it would be near-invisible even under clean geometry. The non-monotonic "poor 1e-7 clock is 9 km *better*" result is a gate accept/reject-count artifact (797/178 vs 719/256), not clock physics. The report admits this. Conclusion: the study yields **no usable Rb-vs-OCXO BOM evidence**; the sweep cannot see the clock because both a dominant geometry error and the common-mode injection design hide it.

**Finding 4 — HIGH / high confidence — "longer legs help 222km→20km" is real convergence but not transferable (confound (d)).** The filter genuinely improves as Doppler accumulates — directionally consistent with D57 — but the *magnitude* is an artifact of the slow-MEO fixture: on the representative LEO constellation, multisat already reached 116 m in a **5-minute** leg. The endurance curve therefore dramatically understates the real system and is confounded with rising handovers (1.0→3.0) and convergence time. It is not a valid quantification of "how much do longer legs buy."

**Finding 5 — MEDIUM / high confidence — brief instruction not followed.** Brief item 1 said reuse "the multi-sat cohort (reuse the multisat fixture/machinery via its public items, or a local realistic fixture)." The study instead built a new, non-representative MEO grid. The likely honest driver (report says "a fixed eight-satellite LEO cohort could not survive endurance intervals"; multisat itself found zero persistent SVs past 15 min, `multisat.rs:711`) is real — but the correct response is to *report that finding* (long fixed-cohort legs are physically unavailable from LEO megaconstellations), not to swap in a slow MEO grid to manufacture a curve.

### Verdict rationale
The brief's stated hinge: "if the endurance study's N=8 geometry is materially worse than multisat's, the two levers are measured on top of an uncontrolled geometry confound and the numbers are not a valid answer… that would be a FAIL needing the study to hold geometry fixed at the multisat good-cohort." That condition is met on three axes at once — MEO-vs-LEO regime (7.5× slower sky motion), per-epoch lowest-ID handover cohort vs fixed persistent cohort, and no GDOP instrumentation. The work is honest, deterministic, in-scope, and gate-green, and the report is commendably candid about its synthetic status — but candor about a confound does not remove it. The leg-duration and clock levers are measured on an uncontrolled, unrepresentative geometry, so the headline numbers are not a valid answer to "how much do longer legs / better clocks buy" for the system in D57.

**Remediation:** hold geometry fixed at the multisat good cohort — reuse the multisat LEO fixture + `persistent_cohort` and report per-tier GDOP demonstrating ~1.8; run the clock sweep on the same 5-min good-geometry cohort so the clock lever is isolated rather than swamped; and if a fixed 8-SV cohort cannot survive long legs on the real LEO constellation, report *that* as the honest endurance finding rather than substituting an MEO grid.
